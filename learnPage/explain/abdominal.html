Abdominal Pain

Identify the cause, treat symptoms safely, and know when to seek emergency care

Introduction

Abdominal pain is a common issue that can range from a mild stomach ache to severe, debilitating cramps. Because the abdomen houses many vital organs—including the stomach, liver, gallbladder, intestines, and appendix—the cause of the pain can vary widely.

While many cases are simply due to indigestion, trapped gas, or minor stomach bugs that can be treated at home, certain symptoms can indicate a serious medical condition. Knowing how to safely relieve discomfort and recognizing the warning signs of an emergency is crucial.

Call Emergency Immediately If:

Do not wait to see a regular doctor if the person experiences any of these critical, life-threatening symptoms:

  • Vomits blood: The vomit may appear bright red or look like dark coffee grounds.
  • Severe difficulty breathing: Breathing is rapid, shallow, or extremely painful.
  • Sudden, agonizing pain: The pain is extremely sharp and appears out of nowhere.
  • Rigid abdomen: The stomach is hard, stiff, and highly tender to the touch (possible appendicitis or organ rupture).
  • Fainting or collapse: The person loses consciousness or becomes unresponsive.

Treating Symptoms at Home

For mild to moderate abdominal pain with a known or non-threatening cause, try the following treatments:

For Heartburn (GERD):

  • Take an over-the-counter antacid or acid reducer to neutralize stomach acid.
  • Avoid eating within 3 hours of going to bed.

For Constipation:

  • Take a mild over-the-counter stool softener or laxative.
  • Increase water intake and eat high-fiber foods.

For General Pain & Cramps:

  • Take acetaminophen (such as Aspirin Free Anacin, Liquiprin, Panadol, or Tylenol) for pain relief.
  • Place a warm heating pad or hot water bottle on your stomach to relax cramping muscles.
  • Sip clear fluids like water, weak tea, or clear broth to stay hydrated, especially if you have had mild diarrhea.
Crucial Medication Warning: Avoid NSAIDs such as aspirin, ibuprofen (Advil, Midol, Motrin), or naproxen (Naprosyn, Aleve, Anaprox, Naprelan). These medications can cause severe stomach irritation, aggravate ulcers, and trigger dangerous internal bleeding.

When to Call a Doctor

Seek medical help if the person is experiencing any of the following symptoms:

  • Has severe abdominal pain or pain that lasts several days.
  • Has nausea, fever, or inability to keep food down for several days.
  • Cannot pass stools, especially if also vomiting (sign of a blockage).
  • Has bloody stools (which may look bright red or tarry black).
  • Has heartburn that isn't relieved by over-the-counter drugs, or lasts longer than two weeks.
  • Has painful urination or a frequent, urgent need to urinate.
  • Has blood in the urine.
  • Had an injury to the abdomen in the days before the pain started.
  • Experiences yellowing of the skin or eyes (jaundice).
  • Is pregnant (or suspects pregnancy) and experiences abdominal pain.

Abdominal Pain DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Drink plenty of clear fluids to prevent dehydration.
  • Eat smaller meals or follow the BRAT diet (Bananas, Rice, Applesauce, Toast) if recovering from a stomach bug.
  • Use a heating pad for muscle cramps.
  • Rest comfortably until the pain subsides.
  • Use Acetaminophen (Tylenol) for pain.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do NOT take NSAIDs like Ibuprofen or Aspirin.
  • Do NOT eat heavy, greasy, fried, or highly spicy foods.
  • Do NOT consume alcohol, caffeine, or carbonated beverages.
  • Do NOT ignore pain that localizes to the lower right side (could be appendicitis).
  • Do NOT force yourself to eat if you feel nauseous.

Prevention Tips

💧

Stay hydrated throughout the day.

🥦

Eat a diet rich in fiber for digestion.

🧼

Wash hands often to avoid stomach bugs.

🍽️

Chew food thoroughly and eat slowly.

🏃

Exercise regularly to prevent constipation.

🧘

Manage stress, which can trigger stomach pain.
