Artificial Respiration

Restoring breathing can save a life

Introduction

Artificial respiration is one of the most important emergency procedures in first aid. It helps provide oxygen to a person who has stopped breathing due to drowning, suffocation, poisoning or other causes. Mouth-to-mouth and mouth-to-nose methods are simple techniques that anyone can learn and apply until professional medical help arrives.

These methods are considered highly effective because they directly deliver air into the lungs and help maintain oxygen supply to vital organs. Prompt action can significantly improve the chances of survival.

Preparing the Victim

  1. Lay the victim flat on the back and kneel beside him.
  2. Loosen tight clothing around the neck and chest.
  3. Remove foreign objects from the mouth and throat.
  4. If water or mucus is present, turn the person sideways to drain it.
  5. Tilt the head backward and lift the chin to open the airway.
  6. Sometimes proper head positioning alone restores breathing.

Mouth-to-Mouth Method

Procedure

  1. Pinch the victim's nostrils closed.
  2. Take a deep breath.
  3. Seal your mouth tightly over the victim's mouth.
  4. Blow air into the lungs until the chest rises.
  5. Allow exhalation and repeat.
  6. Provide one breath every 5 seconds for adults.
  7. For children and infants use gentle breaths.
  8. Continue while there is a pulse or heartbeat.
  9. If revived, keep the victim warm and avoid unnecessary movement.

Mouth-to-Nose Method

Procedure

  1. Maintain head tilt and keep the airway open.
  2. Close the victim's mouth using your hand.
  3. Take a deep breath.
  4. Place your mouth tightly around the nose.
  5. Blow air into the nose until the chest rises.
  6. Open the mouth during exhalation to allow air to escape.
  7. This method is useful when the mouth cannot be opened.

Important Observations

  1. Watch for chest rise and fall.
  2. If the chest does not rise, reassess the airway.
  3. Check again for foreign material blocking the throat.
  4. Continue rescue breathing until normal breathing returns.
  5. Seek immediate medical assistance.
Note: If the stomach becomes swollen, air may have entered the stomach instead of the lungs. Turn the head to one side and clear the mouth before gently pressing the upper abdomen if necessary.

When is Artificial Respiration Required?

Artificial respiration may be required in many emergency situations, including:

  1. Drowning accidents.
  2. Suffocation or choking.
  3. Poisoning.
  4. Electric shock.
  5. Smoke inhalation.
  6. Drug overdose.
  7. Respiratory arrest due to illness.

Quick recognition and immediate action are essential. Maintaining oxygen supply helps prevent brain damage and increases the chances of recovery until trained medical professionals take over.

Safety Tips

  • Always ensure the scene is safe before helping.
  • Call emergency medical services immediately.
  • Maintain an open airway at all times.
  • Continue rescue breathing until the victim breathes normally or medical help arrives.
  • Learn CPR and first aid techniques regularly.
