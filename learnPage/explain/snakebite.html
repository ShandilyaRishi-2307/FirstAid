Snakebites

Stay calm, limit movement and seek emergency medical care immediately

Introduction

Most snakes are harmless to humans, but some species possess venom that can cause serious illness or death. Venomous snakes inject toxins that may damage tissues, affect blood clotting, or interfere with the nervous system. Quick medical treatment greatly improves recovery and reduces complications.

Even if you are unsure whether the snake was venomous, every snakebite should be treated seriously. Stay calm, avoid unnecessary movement, and seek emergency medical attention as soon as possible. Many hospitals carry antivenom, which can neutralize venom and prevent life-threatening complications.

Common Causes

  • Walking through tall grass or bushes.
  • Accidentally stepping on a snake.
  • Attempting to catch or kill snakes.
  • Working outdoors without protective footwear.
  • Camping and hiking in snake habitats.
  • Reaching into dark holes or rock crevices.
  • Handling unknown reptiles.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Puncture marks or fang marks.
  • Pain around the bite area.
  • Rapid swelling and redness.
  • Bruising or discoloration.
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Sweating and weakness.
  • Tingling or numbness.
  • Difficulty breathing in severe cases.

Danger Signs

  • Severe swelling spreading rapidly.
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing.
  • Blurred vision.
  • Bleeding from the gums or wound.
  • Loss of consciousness.
  • Shock or collapse.
  • Paralysis or muscle weakness.
Emergency: Suspected venomous snakebites require immediate medical treatment. Call emergency services right away.

Immediate First Aid Steps

  1. Move away from the snake to avoid another bite.
  2. Remain calm and reassure the casualty.
  3. Call emergency services immediately.
  4. Remove rings, bracelets, watches, and tight clothing.
  5. Keep the affected limb still and below heart level if possible.
  6. Clean the wound gently and cover it with a clean dry dressing.
  7. Keep movement to a minimum to slow venom spread.
  8. Monitor breathing and consciousness.
  9. Be prepared to provide CPR if breathing stops.
  10. Try to remember the snake's color and shape without approaching it.
  11. Transport the casualty to hospital as quickly as possible.

Snakebite First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Stay calm and reassure the person.
  • Call emergency services immediately.
  • Keep the bitten area still.
  • Remove jewelry before swelling develops.
  • Cover the wound with a clean dressing.
  • Observe the snake from a safe distance if possible.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not apply a tourniquet.
  • Do not cut the wound.
  • Do not attempt to suck out venom.
  • Do not apply ice.
  • Do not drink alcohol or caffeine.
  • Do not attempt to catch the snake.

Possible Complications

Venomous snakebites can lead to tissue destruction, severe bleeding, kidney failure, breathing difficulties, shock, paralysis, and death. Infection and permanent scarring may also occur. Delayed treatment increases the risk of serious complications.

Prompt administration of antivenom and supportive medical care significantly improve survival and recovery.

Venomous Snakes in North America

  • Rattlesnakes: Recognized by the rattle at the end of their tails.
  • Copperheads: Pit vipers with triangular heads and slit-like pupils.
  • Water Moccasins (Cottonmouths): Have a white cotton-like lining inside their mouths.
  • Coral Snakes: Characterized by red, yellow, and black bands around the body.
  • Most pit vipers have triangular heads and heat-sensing pits between the eye and nostril.

Prevention and Safety Tips

🥾

Wear boots outdoors.

🚶

Stay on clear paths.

🌿

Avoid tall grass.

🔦

Use a flashlight at night.

✋

Never handle wild snakes.

🏕️

Inspect campsites carefully.

Why Early Treatment Matters

Venom spreads through the bloodstream and lymphatic system. Early medical treatment and antivenom therapy can prevent severe damage to tissues and organs. Delays increase the risk of respiratory failure, shock, and permanent disability.

Keeping the casualty calm and limiting movement slows venom absorption and improves the effectiveness of treatment. Immediate hospital care provides the best chance of a full recovery.
