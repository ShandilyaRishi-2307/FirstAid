CPR (Cardiac Massage)

Immediate action can save a life

Introduction

Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) is an emergency lifesaving technique used when a person's breathing or heartbeat has stopped. It helps maintain blood circulation and oxygen supply to the brain and vital organs until professional medical assistance arrives.

CPR is commonly required during cardiac arrest, drowning, electric shock, choking, suffocation and severe trauma. Learning CPR can greatly improve a victim's chances of survival.

Before Giving CPR

  • Check the scene and ensure it is safe.
  • Tap the person and ask loudly, "Are you OK?"
  • Call emergency medical services immediately.
  • Ask someone to bring an AED if available.
  • Open the airway by tilting the head slightly backward.
  • Check breathing for no more than 10 seconds.
  • If the person is not breathing, begin CPR immediately.

Chest Compressions

  • Place one hand over the other in the center of the chest.
  • Push hard and fast using body weight.
  • Compress at least 2 inches deep.
  • Maintain 100–120 compressions per minute.
  • Allow the chest to fully recoil after each compression.
  • Continue compressions without unnecessary interruptions.

Rescue Breaths

  • Tilt the head back and lift the chin.
  • Pinch the nose closed.
  • Seal your mouth over the victim's mouth.
  • Give two rescue breaths.
  • Watch for chest rise.
  • If the chest does not rise, reposition the head and try again.
  • Look for possible airway obstruction.

Red Cross CPR Steps

  1. Push hard and fast in the center of the chest at a rate of 100–120 compressions per minute.
  2. Deliver two rescue breaths after every 30 compressions.
  3. Continue cycles of compressions and breaths until:
    • The victim starts breathing.
    • An AED becomes available.
    • Emergency personnel arrive.
    • You become too exhausted to continue.

CPR DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Call emergency services immediately.
  • Begin CPR without delay.
  • Push hard and fast.
  • Allow full chest recoil.
  • Use an AED if available.
  • Continue CPR until help arrives.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not stop compressions unnecessarily.
  • Do not bend the arms while compressing.
  • Do not perform CPR on a conscious person.
  • Do not panic.
  • Do not leave the victim alone.
  • Do not give food or water.

Automated External Defibrillator (AED)

An Automated External Defibrillator (AED) is a portable device used to restore normal heart rhythm during cardiac arrest. If available, start CPR while another person retrieves the AED.

Attach the pads according to the instructions and follow the voice prompts provided by the device. The AED automatically analyzes the heart rhythm and advises whether a shock is necessary.

Important: Hands-only CPR consisting of chest compressions alone can save lives when rescue breathing cannot be performed.

Why CPR Is Important

CPR maintains blood flow and oxygen supply to the brain and vital organs during cardiac arrest. Quick intervention significantly improves survival rates and reduces the risk of permanent brain damage.

Regular CPR training and awareness help people remain prepared during emergencies. Even basic knowledge of chest compressions can make a life-saving difference.
