Choking in Adults

Act quickly to clear the airway and restore breathing

Introduction

Choking occurs when food or another object blocks the airway, preventing air from reaching the lungs. The blockage may be partial, allowing the person to cough, or complete, making breathing impossible. Severe choking is a life-threatening emergency that requires immediate action.

If the airway remains blocked, oxygen levels quickly fall and the casualty may become unconscious. Prompt first aid, including back blows and abdominal thrusts, can often remove the obstruction and save a life.

Common Causes

  • Eating too quickly.
  • Large pieces of food becoming stuck.
  • Talking or laughing while eating.
  • Poorly chewed food.
  • Alcohol consumption while eating.
  • Swallowing small objects accidentally.
  • Neurological or swallowing disorders.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Difficulty breathing.
  • Inability to speak or cry out.
  • Weak or ineffective coughing.
  • Clutching the throat.
  • Panic and distress.
  • Blue lips or face.
  • Noisy or high-pitched breathing.
  • Loss of consciousness if untreated.

Danger Signs

  • Unable to cough.
  • Unable to speak.
  • Unable to breathe.
  • Silent attempts to cough.
  • Bluish skin or lips.
  • Collapse or unconsciousness.
  • No response.
Emergency: Severe choking can cause death within minutes. Call emergency services immediately if the airway cannot be cleared.

Immediate First Aid Steps

  1. Ask, "Are you choking?"
  2. If they can cough, encourage them to continue coughing.
  3. Support the person's upper body and help them lean forward.
  4. If coughing fails, give up to five firm back blows between the shoulder blades.
  5. Check whether the obstruction has been removed.
  6. If back blows fail, stand behind them and give up to five abdominal thrusts.
  7. Place your fist above the navel and below the breastbone.
  8. Pull sharply inward and upward.
  9. Alternate five back blows and five abdominal thrusts.
  10. Call emergency services if choking continues.
  11. If the casualty becomes unconscious, begin CPR immediately.

Choking First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Encourage coughing if possible.
  • Help the person lean forward.
  • Give firm back blows.
  • Use abdominal thrusts if needed.
  • Call emergency services promptly.
  • Begin CPR if they become unresponsive.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not slap them randomly.
  • Do not put fingers into the mouth blindly.
  • Do not give food or drink.
  • Do not leave the casualty alone.
  • Do not delay calling for help.
  • Do not stop first aid until help arrives or the object clears.

Possible Complications

Severe choking deprives the brain and body of oxygen. Prolonged airway obstruction may result in brain injury, cardiac arrest, permanent disability, or death. Abdominal thrusts may also occasionally cause internal injuries or broken ribs.

Early treatment greatly reduces the risk of complications and improves survival.

Prevention and Safety Tips

🍽️

Chew food thoroughly.

🥤

Eat slowly and calmly.

🚫

Avoid talking while chewing.

🍇

Cut food into smaller pieces.

👨‍⚕️

Treat swallowing disorders.

🩺

Learn CPR and first aid.

Why Early Treatment Matters

Complete airway obstruction can quickly lead to unconsciousness and cardiac arrest. Immediate action with back blows and abdominal thrusts often removes the obstruction before oxygen levels become critically low.

If the casualty becomes unresponsive, prompt CPR may restore breathing and circulation until emergency medical services arrive. Rapid intervention saves lives and reduces the risk of permanent brain damage.
