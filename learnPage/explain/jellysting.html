Jellyfish Sting

Act quickly to deactivate stingers, relieve pain, and know when to seek emergency care

Introduction

Jellyfish stings are common problems for people swimming, wading or diving in seawaters. The long tentacles trailing from the jellyfish body can inject you with venom from thousands of microscopic barbed stingers called nematocysts.

While many jellyfish stings result only in localized pain and red, irritated marks on the skin, some stings—especially from species like the box jellyfish or Portuguese man-of-war—can cause systemic, life-threatening illness. Knowing the right first aid for the specific region and species is crucial.

When to Call an Ambulance

Immediate emergency medical care is required if any of the following apply:

  • Severe Allergic Reaction: The person displays signs of anaphylaxis, including difficulty breathing, swelling of the face/tongue, or a drop in blood pressure.
  • Box Jellyfish: The sting is known or suspected to be from a highly venomous box jellyfish (common in Indo-Pacific and Australian waters).
  • Large Affected Area: The sting covers more than half of an arm or a leg, or affects sensitive areas like the face or genitals.
Important: If a severe reaction occurs, the person may need to be hospitalized. Anti-venom is available for severe Australian box jellyfish stings and must be administered right away to be most effective.

Immediate First Aid Steps

If the sting is not a life-threatening emergency, follow these steps to neutralize the venom and manage the pain:

  1. Get out of the water: Safely return to shore to avoid further stings and the risk of drowning due to pain or shock.
  2. Wash with seawater: Rinse the area generously with seawater to help deactivate stinging cells. Do not use fresh water.
  3. Remove tentacles: Carefully scrape off any remaining tentacles using the edge of a credit card, a shell, or another stiff plastic object. Do not use your bare hands.
  4. Decontaminate (Tropical Waters): For stings in the Indo-Pacific, Hawaiian box jellyfish, or Portuguese man-of-war, the American Heart Association recommends rinsing the area with vinegar for at least 30 seconds (or a baking soda solution if vinegar is unavailable) to deactivate the stingers.
  5. Apply Heat: After removing tentacles, immerse the affected limb immediately in hot water at 40 to 45°C (104 to 113°F) for at least 20 minutes. A hot shower can be used for other body parts. If hot water is unavailable, cold packs can be used.
Geographic Note: Vinegar treatment is primarily based on research from Indo-Pacific areas. In the North Atlantic, vinegar may actually make symptoms worse depending on the jellyfish species. In those regions, experts recommend simply removing the stinging cells and rinsing with seawater.

Jellyfish Sting DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Get out of the water immediately.
  • Rinse the area with seawater.
  • Remove tentacles with a rigid plastic edge (like a credit card).
  • Use hot water immersion (40-45°C) to relieve pain.
  • Use vinegar for tropical box jellyfish stings.
  • Seek emergency care for breathing issues or large stings.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not rinse with fresh water (it causes stinging cells to fire).
  • Do not rub or scratch the affected area.
  • Do not touch tentacles with your bare hands.
  • Do not apply ice directly to the skin initially (use heat first if possible).
  • Do not urinate on the sting (this is a dangerous myth and can worsen the pain).
  • Do not use vinegar for North Atlantic jellyfish stings.

Treating Discomfort

Once the initial stingers are neutralized and removed, you can manage ongoing pain and skin reactions:

  • Use mild over-the-counter hydrocortisone cream to reduce swelling and redness.
  • Take an oral antihistamine to help relieve severe itching.
  • Apply ice packs or take over-the-counter pain relievers (like ibuprofen or acetaminophen) for residual welts and pain.

Aftercare & Follow-Up

Proper wound care prevents secondary infections:

  • Clean any open sores 3 times a day with mild soap and warm water.
  • Apply an over-the-counter antibiotic ointment.
  • Cover with a sterile bandage if needed to protect the skin from dirt and friction.
  • Monitor the area for several days; if red streaks develop or the wound becomes hot to the touch, consult a doctor immediately.

Prevention and Beach Safety

🚩

Obey beach warning flags and signs.

🩱

Wear protective stinger suits or wet suits.

🏊‍♂️

Swim near a lifeguard on duty.

🏖️

Avoid swimming during jellyfish season.

🚫

Never touch dead jellyfish on the beach.

🧴

Consider specialized jellyfish repellent lotions.
