Shock

Early treatment can save a life

Introduction

Shock (not to be confused with emotional shock) is a life-threatening condition that occurs when the body does not receive enough blood flow. As a result, cells do not get enough oxygen and nutrients to function properly. If untreated, shock can lead to damage of vital organs such as the brain, kidneys and heart.

Shock often develops after severe injuries, blood loss, heart attacks, allergic reactions, burns, or infections. Immediate first aid and rapid medical attention are essential to improve survival.

Causes of Shock

  • Heart problems such as heart attack or heart failure.
  • Severe internal or external bleeding.
  • Loss of body fluids due to dehydration, vomiting, diarrhea or burns.
  • Severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis).
  • Serious infections.
  • Major trauma and accidents.

Any condition that reduces blood circulation can cause shock. Therefore, treating the underlying cause is equally important.

Signs and Symptoms

If you suspect shock, look for these warning signs:

  1. Pale appearance (pallor).
  2. Cold and clammy skin.
  3. Fast, weak pulse.
  4. Rapid, shallow breathing.
  5. Yawning or sighing.
  6. Confusion or restlessness.
  7. Loss of consciousness in severe cases.

Additional Symptoms

  • Weakness and dizziness.
  • Thirst.
  • Nausea or vomiting.
  • Bluish lips or fingernails.
  • Anxiety or fear.
  • Reduced alertness.
  • Fainting.
Important: Shock can become fatal very quickly. Always seek emergency medical help immediately.

What You Need To Do

  1. Lay the person down with the head low and legs raised to improve blood flow to the brain.
  2. Call emergency services immediately and explain the cause if known.
  3. Loosen tight clothing around the neck, chest and waist.
  4. Keep the person warm using a blanket or coat.
  5. Reassure and comfort the casualty because fear and pain can worsen shock.
  6. Monitor breathing, pulse and level of consciousness continuously.
  7. If unconscious, open the airway and be prepared to give CPR if breathing stops.

Shock First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Call for emergency medical help.
  • Keep the person warm and comfortable.
  • Raise the legs if there are no fractures.
  • Check breathing and pulse regularly.
  • Treat severe bleeding immediately.
  • Stay with the person until help arrives.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not give food or drink.
  • Do not allow the person to stand or walk.
  • Do not leave the victim alone.
  • Do not raise the legs if spinal injury is suspected.
  • Do not move seriously injured victims unnecessarily.
  • Do not delay medical treatment.

Prevention and Safety Tips

Many cases of shock can be prevented by avoiding accidents, staying hydrated, treating severe bleeding promptly, managing heart conditions properly and seeking immediate treatment for allergic reactions or infections.

Learning first aid and CPR can help you act confidently during emergencies and greatly improve the chances of survival.

Emergency Warning: If a person becomes unconscious and stops breathing normally, begin CPR immediately and continue until professional help arrives.
