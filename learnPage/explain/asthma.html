Asthma Attacks

Recognize the signs, assist with medication, and know when to get emergency help

Asthma and Hayfever

Hayfever is a major risk factor for asthma, and the two are often experienced side by side. Poorly treated hayfever can significantly increase the threat of an asthma attack.

We recommend regularly checking pollen counts and staying on top of medication to keep asthma attacks at bay. If you’re asthmatic and you know hayfever affects you, keep your inhaler with you at all times, especially during high-pollen seasons.

What is an Asthma Attack?

An asthma attack happens when the lung muscles spasm and the airway swells. This causes the airways to become incredibly narrow, making it very hard for the person to breathe.

Common Triggers: These attacks can be caused by many different things, including seasonal allergies (like hayfever), exposure to smoke, or strenuous physical exercise.

How to Recognise an Attack

Look out for the following signs and symptoms:

  • Difficulty breathing and catching their breath.
  • Wheezing, specifically on the exhale.
  • Difficulty speaking, sometimes reduced to whispering.
  • Visible distress, panic, and anxiety.
  • Persistent coughing.
  • A grey-blue tinge to the lips, earlobes, and nailbeds (indicating lack of oxygen).

Know What to Do

Knowing first aid can help those around you stay safe. Here’s what you need to do if someone is having an asthma attack:

  1. Keep calm: Panicking could make their breathing worse. Reassure them and encourage slow, deep breaths.
  2. Positioning: Help them into a comfortable breathing position (usually sitting upright, leaning slightly forward).
  3. Use medication: Encourage them to use their inhaler if they have one (typically a blue reliever inhaler).
  4. Dosing: A mild attack should ease within a few minutes. If it doesn’t, encourage them to take one or two puffs every two minutes, up to a maximum of 10 puffs.
  5. Monitor: Constantly monitor their vital signs: breathing, pulse, and responsiveness.
Emergency: Call an ambulance immediately if the inhaler has no effect, if they don't have an inhaler, or if the person is becoming physically exhausted from trying to breathe.

Asthma First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Keep the person calm and reassured.
  • Help them sit up comfortably.
  • Help them find and use their reliever inhaler.
  • Wait 2 minutes between taking extra puffs (up to 10).
  • Call an ambulance if their condition doesn't improve.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do NOT panic or show anxiety (it will transfer to them).
  • Do NOT lay the person flat down on their back.
  • Do NOT leave the person alone during an attack.
  • Do NOT ignore grey or blue coloring in the lips/nails.
  • Do NOT delay calling an ambulance if they are exhausted.

Asthma Management & Prevention

🌬️

Check daily pollen counts.

🎒

Always carry your reliever inhaler.

🚭

Avoid cigarette smoke and fumes.

🧹

Keep homes free of dust and mold.

🏃

Warm up before exercising.

👨‍⚕️

Attend regular asthma reviews.
