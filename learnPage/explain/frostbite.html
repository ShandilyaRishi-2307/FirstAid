Frostbite

Protect frozen tissue and prevent permanent damage

Introduction

Frostbite occurs when skin and underlying tissues freeze because of prolonged exposure to very low temperatures. It commonly affects the fingers, toes, ears, nose, and cheeks because these areas are farthest from the heart and receive less blood flow.

Severe frostbite can permanently damage tissues and may lead to gangrene, where tissue dies because of a loss of blood supply. People suffering from frostbite often also have hypothermia, so both conditions should be considered and treated promptly.

Common Causes

  • Exposure to freezing temperatures.
  • Cold and windy weather.
  • Wet clothing or wet skin.
  • High altitude environments.
  • Long periods outdoors without proper protection.
  • Reduced blood circulation.
  • Inability to move around.
  • Alcohol or drug use causing impaired judgment.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Pins and needles sensation.
  • Pale skin and numbness.
  • Hardening and stiffening of the skin.
  • White, blotchy, or blue discoloration.
  • Loss of feeling in the affected area.
  • Clumsiness or inability to use fingers.
  • Red, painful skin and blistering during recovery.
  • Black tissue if gangrene develops.

Danger Signs

  • Loss of sensation.
  • Severe blistering.
  • Black or dead tissue.
  • Associated hypothermia.
  • Confusion or drowsiness.
  • Difficulty moving affected limbs.
  • Signs of shock.
Emergency: Severe frostbite requires urgent hospital treatment to prevent permanent tissue damage.

Immediate First Aid Steps

  1. Move the casualty indoors or to a warm shelter.
  2. Encourage them to place hands in their armpits.
  3. Remove rings, gloves, boots and tight clothing.
  4. Warm the affected area gently with body heat.
  5. Never rub or massage frozen skin.
  6. If there is no risk of refreezing, immerse the area in warm water around 40°C (104°F).
  7. Dry carefully and apply a sterile gauze dressing.
  8. Elevate the limb to reduce swelling.
  9. Arrange transport to hospital while keeping the limb protected.

Frostbite First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Move the person to a warm place.
  • Use body heat or warm water for rewarming.
  • Remove tight items like rings.
  • Cover with sterile dressings.
  • Keep the affected limb elevated.
  • Seek medical treatment quickly.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not rub or massage the area.
  • Do not use direct heat sources.
  • Do not break blisters.
  • Do not walk on frostbitten feet.
  • Do not thaw if refreezing is possible.
  • Do not delay hospital treatment.

Stages of Frostbite

  • Frostnip: Mild form causing tingling and redness.
  • Superficial Frostbite: White skin with numbness and hardening.
  • Deep Frostbite: Damage to muscles, tendons and bones.
  • Gangrene: Tissue death causing black discoloration.

Possible Complications

Untreated frostbite can lead to permanent nerve damage, chronic pain, infection, loss of movement, and gangrene. Severe cases may require surgery or amputation of affected body parts.

Associated hypothermia can also become life-threatening and requires immediate medical care.

People Most at Risk

  • Mountaineers and hikers.
  • Outdoor workers.
  • Homeless individuals.
  • Elderly people.
  • People with diabetes or poor circulation.
  • Children and infants.
  • People under the influence of alcohol or drugs.

Prevention and Safety Tips

🧥

Wear warm layers.

🧤

Protect hands and feet.

🧦

Keep clothing dry.

🏠

Seek shelter regularly.

☕

Stay hydrated and nourished.

🌬️

Avoid prolonged wind exposure.

Frostbite vs Hypothermia

Frostbite affects specific body parts and causes tissue freezing, while hypothermia occurs when the entire body temperature falls dangerously low. A person with frostbite may also have hypothermia, which causes shivering, confusion, slurred speech and drowsiness.

Both conditions are medical emergencies and should be treated simultaneously to prevent life-threatening complications.

Why Early Treatment Matters

Rapid and gentle warming improves blood flow and helps prevent permanent tissue damage. Delays in treatment increase the risk of nerve injury, infection, gangrene and loss of limbs.

Prompt hospital care gives the best chance of preserving tissue and ensuring complete recovery.
