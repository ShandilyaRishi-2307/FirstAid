Fever

Monitor temperature, keep cool, and prevent complications

Introduction

A fever is an increase in body temperature above the normal level of 37°C (98.6°F). It is usually caused by infections or illnesses such as colds, sore throats, ear infections, influenza, or chickenpox. Fever is a natural response that helps the body fight infection.

Although fevers are common and usually harmless, high temperatures can be dangerous, especially in young children. Temperatures above 39°C (102.2°F) may trigger febrile seizures (fits) and require medical attention. Monitoring symptoms and keeping the person comfortable are important parts of first aid.

Common Causes

  • Viral infections such as colds or flu.
  • Bacterial infections.
  • Sore throat and ear infections.
  • Chickenpox and other childhood illnesses.
  • Heat exhaustion or dehydration.
  • Inflammatory conditions.
  • Reaction to certain vaccinations.
  • Side effects of some medications.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Body temperature above 37°C (98.6°F).
  • Pale skin during the early stage.
  • Shivering and goose pimples.
  • Chattering teeth and feeling cold.
  • Hot flushed skin and sweating later.
  • Headache and body aches.
  • Fatigue and weakness.
  • Loss of appetite.

Danger Signs

  • Temperature above 39°C (102.2°F).
  • Difficulty breathing.
  • Severe headache or stiff neck.
  • Confusion or unusual behavior.
  • Seizures (fits).
  • Persistent vomiting.
  • Loss of consciousness.
Important: Seek urgent medical help if symptoms worsen or if a child develops seizures.

Immediate First Aid Steps

  1. Measure body temperature with a thermometer.
  2. Keep the person comfortable and in a cool environment.
  3. Use light clothing or a light blanket.
  4. Encourage plenty of cool fluids.
  5. Allow the person to rest.
  6. Give paracetamol in the recommended dose if necessary.
  7. Do not give aspirin to anyone under 16 years old.
  8. Monitor breathing, pulse and responsiveness.
  9. Seek medical advice if symptoms worsen or temperature exceeds 39°C.

Fever First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Give plenty of fluids.
  • Allow the person to rest.
  • Use a thermometer regularly.
  • Keep clothing light and comfortable.
  • Follow medication instructions carefully.
  • Seek medical advice if concerned.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not overdress the person.
  • Do not force food.
  • Do not give aspirin to children.
  • Do not ignore worsening symptoms.
  • Do not leave someone having a seizure alone.
  • Do not delay seeking emergency care when necessary.

Possible Complications

High fever can lead to dehydration, seizures, confusion, and worsening of the underlying infection. In severe cases, untreated infections causing fever may spread throughout the body and become life-threatening.

Children are particularly vulnerable to febrile seizures when temperatures rise rapidly. Prompt treatment and hydration help reduce the risk of complications.

Special Care for Children

  • Monitor their temperature regularly.
  • Offer breast milk, formula, or water frequently.
  • Dress them in lightweight clothing.
  • Never use cold baths or ice packs.
  • Watch for signs of dehydration.
  • Seek medical advice if fever lasts more than two days.
  • Call emergency services if a seizure occurs.

Prevention and Safety Tips

🧼

Wash hands regularly.

💧

Drink plenty of fluids.

😴

Get adequate rest.

💉

Keep vaccinations up to date.

🥗

Maintain a healthy diet.

🤒

Treat infections promptly.

When to Seek Medical Help

Medical attention should be sought if the fever is above 39°C (102.2°F), lasts for several days, or is accompanied by difficulty breathing, seizures, confusion, severe headache, or persistent vomiting.

Infants younger than three months with any fever should be assessed by a healthcare professional immediately. Early treatment helps prevent serious complications and ensures proper recovery.

Why Early Treatment Matters

Most fevers improve with rest, hydration, and treatment of the underlying cause. Monitoring symptoms and keeping the person comfortable helps the body recover more effectively.

Recognizing warning signs early can prevent dehydration, seizures, and serious infections. Prompt medical care greatly improves outcomes and reduces the risk of complications.
