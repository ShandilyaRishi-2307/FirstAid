Burns and Scalds

Cool the burn quickly and prevent further damage

Introduction

Burns and scalds are injuries caused by heat that damage the skin and underlying tissues. Burns are commonly caused by dry heat such as fire, hot metals, electricity, chemicals, or excessive sun exposure. Scalds result from wet heat such as hot liquids or steam.

The severity of a burn depends on its depth, size, and location. Immediate cooling of the affected area reduces tissue damage and improves healing. Severe burns can lead to shock, dehydration, infection, and life-threatening complications, requiring urgent medical care.

Types of Burns

  • First-Degree Burn: Red, painful skin without blisters.
  • Second-Degree Burn: Red skin with blisters and swelling.
  • Third-Degree Burn: White, charred, or leathery skin with severe tissue damage.
  • Chemical Burn: Caused by acids or alkalis.
  • Electrical Burn: Damage caused by electric current.
  • Sunburn: Excessive exposure to ultraviolet radiation.

Common Signs and Symptoms

  • Redness of the skin.
  • Pain and tenderness.
  • Swelling.
  • Blister formation.
  • Peeling skin.
  • White or charred appearance.
  • Difficulty moving the affected area.
  • Signs of shock in severe burns.

Serious Burns Need Urgent Care

  • Burn larger than the casualty's hand.
  • Burns affecting face, hands, feet or genitals.
  • Deep burns involving several skin layers.
  • Chemical or electrical burns.
  • Difficulty breathing.
  • Loss of consciousness.
  • Associated injuries or fractures.
Important: Call emergency medical services immediately for severe burns.

Immediate First Aid Steps

  1. Move the casualty away from the source of heat.
  2. Cool the burn under cool running water for at least 20 minutes.
  3. Do not use ice, butter, creams or toothpaste.
  4. Remove rings, watches and tight clothing.
  5. Do not remove clothing stuck to the burn.
  6. Cover the burn with cling film or a sterile non-fluffy dressing.
  7. Keep the person warm to prevent shock.
  8. Seek medical help for serious burns.

Burn First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Cool the burn immediately.
  • Remove jewelry near the injury.
  • Cover with a clean dressing.
  • Keep the casualty comfortable.
  • Treat for shock if necessary.
  • Seek medical advice when unsure.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not apply ice.
  • Do not burst blisters.
  • Do not apply ointments or toothpaste.
  • Do not pull off stuck clothing.
  • Do not touch the wound unnecessarily.
  • Do not delay seeking help for severe burns.

Possible Complications

Severe burns can cause dehydration, infection, shock, breathing difficulties, permanent scarring, joint stiffness, and organ failure. Extensive burns may become life-threatening without prompt medical treatment.

Infection is one of the most serious complications, which is why proper cleaning and covering of the wound are essential.

Special Types of Burns

  • Chemical Burns: Flush with running water for at least 20 minutes and remove contaminated clothing.
  • Electrical Burns: Turn off the power source before touching the victim and seek immediate medical help.
  • Sunburn: Move to shade, cool the skin, drink plenty of fluids, and avoid further sun exposure.

Prevention and Safety Tips

🔥

Keep away from open flames.

☕

Handle hot liquids carefully.

🧤

Use protective equipment.

⚡

Follow electrical safety.

☀️

Protect skin from sunlight.

👶

Keep children away from heat sources.

Why Early Treatment Matters

Cooling the burn immediately helps stop the burning process and limits damage to deeper tissues. Proper first aid reduces pain, decreases the risk of infection, and improves healing.

Severe burns can quickly become life-threatening because of fluid loss and shock. Early treatment and timely medical care greatly improve recovery and reduce complications.
