Fishhook Removal

Learn the safe technique to remove a fishhook and prevent serious infection

Introduction

Fishing is a widespread leisurely activity that rarely results in an emergency. However, trauma from a fishhook piercing the skin is fairly common.

Because of the barb at the end of the hook, simply pulling the hook backward out of the skin will cause severe tearing and tissue damage. Using the correct "push-through" technique with appropriate tools is crucial to minimizing injury before seeking medical evaluation for infection control.

First Aid: The Push-Through Method

If the hook is embedded in an area that is safe to treat (not the eye, face, or near an artery), use the following guidelines to remove a fishhook:

  1. Push Through: Use pliers or forceps to push the hook gently through the skin, following the natural curve of the hook until the barbed end pokes out the other side.
  2. Cut the Barb: Use heavy-duty wire cutters or pliers to snip off the barb so that the remaining unbarbed portion of the fishhook is smooth.
  3. Back it Out: Carefully pull the unbarbed portion of the fishhook backwards out through the original entry wound.
  4. Clean, but Do Not Close: Clean the wound thoroughly with soap and water, but do not close or seal the wound tightly. Puncture wounds need to drain to prevent deep infections.
  5. Follow Up: Follow standard wound care guidelines and consult a doctor to start antibiotics immediately to prevent marine-borne bacterial infections.

When to Seek Medical Care

A fishhook wound can lead to a serious, aggressive infection due to the bacteria found in lakes, rivers, oceans, and on fish. Always keep the following in mind:

  • Consult a Doctor: Be certain to consult a doctor about available medications for the treatment of fishhook wounds. You will likely be prescribed antibiotics.
  • Tetanus Vaccination: People who experience a fishhook puncture should be immunized for tetanus if more than 5 years have passed since their last inoculation.
  • Dangerous Locations: If the hook is lodged in the eye, face, neck, or deep inside a joint/bone, do NOT attempt removal. Tape it securely in place so it doesn't move and go to the emergency room immediately.
Emergency Alert: Never attempt the push-through method if you feel resistance against a bone, or if removing it could damage vital blood vessels or nerves. Let emergency professionals handle complex extractions.

Fishhook Removal DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Wash your hands before touching the wound.
  • Use pliers to push the hook through its natural curve.
  • Cut the barb off completely before pulling the hook back out.
  • Leave the wound open to drain after washing it.
  • Check your tetanus vaccine status immediately.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do NOT pull a barbed hook backward out of the skin (it will tear the tissue).
  • Do NOT close the wound tightly with glue or butterfly stitches.
  • Do NOT attempt to remove hooks near the eyes, face, or neck.
  • Do NOT ignore signs of infection like red streaks, heat, or throbbing pain.

Prevention Tips on the Water

🥽

Always wear protective safety glasses/sunglasses.

👀

Look behind you before casting your line.

🛠️

Use long pliers to extract hooks from fish.

🧰

Keep tackle boxes closed when not in use.

👟

Wear closed-toe shoes on boats and docks.

🎣

Consider pinching barbs down for catch-and-release.
