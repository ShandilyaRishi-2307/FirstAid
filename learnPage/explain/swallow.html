Swallowing Tongue

Keeping the airway open can save a life

Introduction

The term "swallowing the tongue" is commonly used to describe situations in which an unconscious person's tongue falls backward and partially blocks the airway. Although it is impossible to actually swallow the tongue, the resulting obstruction can interfere with breathing and may become life-threatening.

These situations are commonly seen after sports injuries, seizures, accidents, fainting episodes, or other medical emergencies involving loss of consciousness. Immediate first aid helps restore airflow and prevent complications caused by lack of oxygen.

What Really Happens?

The tongue is a muscular organ attached to the lower jaw and cannot be swallowed. During unconsciousness, the muscles relax and the tongue may fall backward, narrowing or blocking the airway.

Common Causes

  • Head injuries and concussion.
  • Epileptic seizures and convulsions.
  • Loss of consciousness.
  • Reduced blood circulation.
  • Heart rhythm abnormalities.
  • Low blood sugar (Hypoglycemia).
  • Electrolyte imbalance.
  • Severe trauma and accidents.

Warning Signs

  • Difficulty breathing.
  • Noisy breathing or snoring sounds.
  • Bluish lips and fingertips.
  • Loss of consciousness.
  • Weak pulse.
  • Pale skin.
  • Seizures or convulsions.
  • Unresponsiveness.
  • Sudden collapse.

Danger Signs

Airway blockage can quickly reduce oxygen supply to the brain and vital organs. Brain damage may begin within a few minutes if breathing is not restored.

Important: Call emergency medical services immediately and ensure the airway remains open.

First Aid Measures

  1. Stay calm and reassure people nearby.
  2. Tilt the head backward and raise the chin.
  3. Move the lower jaw forward.
  4. Open the mouth carefully and ensure breathing is possible.
  5. Turn the casualty to the recovery position if breathing normally.
  6. Do not place fingers deep into the mouth.
  7. Seek emergency medical help immediately.
  8. If breathing stops, begin CPR.

Recovery Position

  • Turn the person onto one side.
  • Keep the head tilted slightly backward.
  • Allow saliva or vomit to drain naturally.
  • Keep the airway open.
  • Monitor breathing continuously.
  • Protect the head and neck.
  • Keep the person warm.

When CPR Is Required

  • If breathing stops completely.
  • If there is no pulse.
  • Begin chest compressions immediately.
  • Give rescue breaths if trained.
  • Continue until medical help arrives.
  • Use an AED if available.
  • Never stop CPR unnecessarily.

First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Open the airway using head tilt and chin lift.
  • Call emergency services.
  • Place breathing victims in the recovery position.
  • Monitor pulse and breathing.
  • Start CPR if necessary.
  • Keep the person calm and warm.
  • Provide medical information to healthcare professionals.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not panic.
  • Do not forcefully pull the tongue.
  • Do not place fingers deep inside the mouth.
  • Do not give food or drinks to unconscious people.
  • Do not leave the person alone.
  • Do not delay emergency treatment.

Prevention and Safety Measures

Wear protective helmets during sports.

Manage heart diseases properly.

Control blood sugar levels.

Treat seizures and neurological disorders.

Seek medical attention after head injuries.

Learn CPR and first aid skills.

Why Immediate Action Matters

Most cases referred to as "swallowing the tongue" are actually airway obstructions caused by relaxation of the tongue during unconsciousness. Immediate airway management and prompt medical attention can prevent suffocation and brain injury.

Learning basic first aid techniques such as the recovery position and CPR enables anyone to provide life-saving assistance until professional help arrives.
