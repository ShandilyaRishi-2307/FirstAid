Signs of Labor

Recognize the signs, know when to go to the hospital, and stay calm

Introduction

Labor is a natural process, but it can be an anxious time, especially for first-time parents. The onset of labor is characterized by a series of physical changes as the body prepares to deliver the baby.

Not every woman experiences the same symptoms or follows the same timeline. Recognizing the difference between "false labor" (Braxton Hicks) and true labor is essential. It's important to monitor contractions, watch for specific bodily cues, and know exactly when it’s time to call your healthcare provider or head to the hospital.

Call Emergency If:

While labor is normal, certain symptoms indicate a medical emergency. Call for an ambulance (or head to the emergency room immediately) if the woman experiences:

  • Heavy Vaginal Bleeding: Bleeding that is soaking through a pad quickly (more than just a "bloody show" or spotting).
  • Severe Stomach or Pelvic Pain: Sharp, intense, and unremitting pain that does not ebb and flow like a contraction.
  • Seizures: Any convulsive activity.
  • Loss of Consciousness: Fainting or unresponsiveness.
  • Cord Prolapse: The umbilical cord is bulging into the vagina, often accompanied by a sudden gush or leaking of fluid. (This is a critical emergency—get on hands and knees to relieve pressure and call 911 immediately).

Signs That Labor Has Begun

Look for these progressive signs that your body is actively entering labor:

  • True Contractions: They come at regular intervals, increase in frequency and intensity, and do not stop when you change positions, rest, or drink water. They often feel like a dull ache in the lower back radiating to the front abdomen, or heavy pelvic pressure.
  • Water Breaking: A sudden gush or a steady, uncontrollable trickle of fluid when the amniotic membrane ruptures.
  • Bloody Show: The loss of the mucus plug, which presents as pink, brown, or slightly bloody mucus discharged from the vagina.
  • Lightening (Dropping): The fetus lowers into the pelvis. This eases breathing but increases pressure on the bladder, causing more frequent urination.
  • Nesting Instinct: A sudden, uncontrollable burst of energy to clean and organize right before labor starts.

When to Call a Health Care Provider

Always consult your doctor or midwife, but standard guidelines for calling include:

  • First Pregnancy (The 5-1-1 Rule): Contractions are very uncomfortable, lasting at least 1 minute, and are coming every 5 minutes for at least 1 hour.
  • Subsequent Pregnancies: Labor often progresses faster. Call when contractions are coming every 7 to 10 minutes for an hour.
  • Water Breaks: Even if there are no contractions. Note the time, color, and odor of the fluid. She should immediately avoid baths, douching, tampons, or sexual intercourse to prevent infection.
  • Vaginal Bleeding: Any bleeding that is heavier than standard spotting or the mucus plug.
  • Decreased Fetal Movement: If you notice a sudden drop in the baby's activity levels.
Note: Timing guidelines may vary based on your personal medical history. Always ask your specific health care provider what parameters they recommend for you.

Labor DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Time your contractions (start to start) using an app or a watch.
  • Stay hydrated by sipping water or clear fluids in early labor.
  • Rest and conserve energy during the early, mild stages of labor.
  • Practice deep, rhythmic breathing exercises.
  • Call your birthing center or hospital before leaving.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do NOT panic; stay as calm and relaxed as possible.
  • Do NOT eat heavy, greasy meals during active labor (it may cause nausea/vomiting).
  • Do NOT take a tub bath if your water has broken (risk of infection).
  • Do NOT insert anything into the vagina (no tampons or douching).
  • Do NOT drive yourself to the hospital.
