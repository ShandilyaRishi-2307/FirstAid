Bee and Wasp Stings

Treat the sting, manage the pain, and recognize allergic reactions

Introduction

Treating bee and wasp stings depends entirely on their severity. For the vast majority of people, a sting causes only temporary pain and localized swelling that can be managed at home.

However, the majority of problems that require urgent medical attention come from an allergic reaction to the venom. In most cases, complications from that reaction respond well to medications—when given in time. It is crucial to know the difference between a normal local reaction and a systemic allergic response.

Bees vs. Wasps: What's the Difference?

While their stings cause similar reactions, there is a mechanical difference in how these insects sting:

  • Honeybees: They have barbed stingers. When they sting, the stinger and venom sack are torn from the bee's body and left in your skin (killing the bee).
  • Wasps, Hornets, and Yellow Jackets: Their stingers are smooth. They do not leave their stingers behind, meaning a single insect can sting you multiple times in rapid succession.
Note: Because bees leave their venom sack behind, removing the stinger as fast as possible is the single most important step to minimize the amount of venom injected.

Home First Aid Treatment

Most insect stings for someone who is not allergic need no more than basic first aid:

  1. Remove the stinger: Act immediately. Experts recommend scraping out the stinger with a stiff edge, like a credit card or a fingernail.
  2. Wash the site: Clean the sting site thoroughly with soap and water.
  3. Apply Ice: Wrap ice in a towel (do not put ice directly on the skin) and apply it for 20 minutes once every hour as needed for mild relief.
  4. Treat Itching & Swelling: Take an antihistamine such as diphenhydramine (Benadryl) or loratadine (Claritin). Placing hydrocortisone cream on the sting can also help relieve local redness.
  5. Pain Relief: Take ibuprofen (Motrin) or acetaminophen (Tylenol) as needed.

Allergic Reactions & When to Get Help

If you know you are allergic, or if you've had a severe reaction in the past, seek immediate medical help. Watch for these signs of anaphylaxis:

  • Difficulty breathing or wheezing.
  • Swelling of the lips, tongue, face, or throat.
  • Dizziness, fainting, or a drop in blood pressure.
  • Hives or rash spreading well beyond the sting site.
  • Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea.
Emergency Action: If you have been prescribed an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) for an allergic reaction, always carry two with you. Use it immediately as directed and call an ambulance.

Professional Medical Treatment

If you need to visit a doctor or emergency room, your treatment will depend on your symptoms:

  • No Allergic Symptoms: You may require only local wound care (cleaning and applying antibiotic ointment). Any remaining stingers will be removed. You may be given an oral antihistamine and pain relievers. If your tetanus immunization is not current (more than 10 years), you will receive a booster shot.
  • Mild Allergic Symptoms: If you have a rash and itching over your body but no breathing problems, you may be treated with an antihistamine and possibly steroids to reduce inflammation.
  • Severe Allergic Symptoms: In cases of anaphylaxis, the doctor will give you an epinephrine (adrenaline) injection immediately. Treatment may even be started at the scene or in the ambulance by paramedics. You will require observation in the emergency department until your vitals are stable.

Bee & Wasp Sting DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Scrape the stinger off immediately (within seconds if possible).
  • Wash the area with soap and water.
  • Apply cold compresses or ice wrapped in a cloth.
  • Take an over-the-counter antihistamine.
  • Use an EpiPen immediately if you experience systemic allergic symptoms.
  • Get a tetanus booster if you haven't had one in 10 years.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do NOT pinch or squeeze the stinger with tweezers (this forces more venom into the skin).
  • Do NOT scratch the sting site, as this can introduce bacteria and cause an infection.
  • Do NOT swat aggressively at flying bees/wasps; it makes them more likely to sting.
  • Do NOT ignore trouble breathing or facial swelling.

Prevention and Safety Tips

Avoid further stings by staying out of infested areas and following these tips:

👖

Wear light-colored, protective clothing.

🧴

Use insect repellent when outdoors.

🥤

Cover sweet drinks and food outside.

🌺

Avoid wearing strong perfumes or floral scents.

👟

Don't walk barefoot in the grass.

🏃

Walk away calmly; don't swat or run wildly.
