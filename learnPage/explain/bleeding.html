Severe Bleeding

Control blood loss quickly and prevent shock

Introduction

Severe bleeding is a medical emergency that can rapidly become life-threatening. Excessive blood loss may lead to shock, unconsciousness, and even death if not controlled quickly. Bleeding may result from deep cuts, accidents, penetrating injuries, or damaged blood vessels.

Prompt first aid aims to stop the bleeding, protect the casualty from infection, and maintain circulation until professional medical help arrives. If an object is embedded in the wound, it should never be removed because it may be helping to reduce blood loss.

Common Causes

  • Deep cuts and lacerations.
  • Road traffic accidents.
  • Industrial or workplace injuries.
  • Knife wounds and penetrating injuries.
  • Broken bones damaging blood vessels.
  • Animal bites.
  • Sports injuries.
  • Gunshot or crush injuries.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Heavy or continuous bleeding.
  • Bright red or dark blood loss.
  • Pale, cold, clammy skin.
  • Weakness and dizziness.
  • Rapid pulse.
  • Restlessness or anxiety.
  • Feeling thirsty.
  • Loss of consciousness in severe cases.

Signs of Shock

  • Pale, cold and sweaty skin.
  • Rapid, weak pulse.
  • Fast and shallow breathing.
  • Dizziness or fainting.
  • Confusion and anxiety.
  • Blue lips or fingertips.
  • Loss of responsiveness.
Emergency: Severe bleeding can lead to shock very quickly. Call emergency services immediately.

Immediate First Aid Steps

  1. Wear disposable gloves if available.
  2. Expose the wound by removing or cutting clothing.
  3. If an object is embedded, leave it in place.
  4. Apply pressure around the object using pads or dressings.
  5. If no object is present, apply direct pressure with a sterile dressing.
  6. Secure the dressing firmly with a bandage.
  7. Lay the casualty down and raise the legs if shock develops.
  8. Keep checking breathing, pulse and level of response.
  9. If unconscious, open the airway and prepare for CPR if necessary.

Embedded Objects in Wounds

Never remove objects such as knives, metal fragments, or glass from a wound. The object may be acting like a plug and removing it could cause severe bleeding. Instead, apply pressure on either side using dressings or clean cloths and seek urgent medical help.

Severe Bleeding First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Apply firm pressure to the wound.
  • Wear gloves if possible.
  • Call emergency services immediately.
  • Treat the casualty for shock.
  • Keep monitoring breathing and pulse.
  • Support injured limbs with pillows or slings.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not remove embedded objects.
  • Do not remove the first dressing if soaked.
  • Do not apply unnecessary pressure over protruding objects.
  • Do not allow the casualty to stand or walk.
  • Do not give food or drink if surgery may be needed.
  • Do not leave the casualty alone.

Types of Bleeding

  • Arterial Bleeding: Bright red blood that spurts with each heartbeat.
  • Venous Bleeding: Dark red blood flowing steadily.
  • Capillary Bleeding: Slow oozing from surface wounds.
  • Internal Bleeding: Hidden blood loss inside the body requiring emergency care.

Possible Complications

Uncontrolled blood loss may cause shock, organ failure, permanent disability, infection, and death. Severe injuries can also damage nerves, muscles, and blood vessels.

Rapid first aid and early medical treatment greatly improve survival and reduce complications.

Prevention and Safety Tips

🧤

Use protective equipment.

🚗

Follow road safety rules.

⚙️

Handle tools carefully.

🩹

🏃

☎️

Why Early Treatment Matters

Blood carries oxygen and nutrients throughout the body. Losing large amounts of blood prevents vital organs from functioning properly and can lead to shock within minutes.

Quick control of bleeding, treatment for shock, and rapid access to emergency care can save lives and prevent serious complications.
