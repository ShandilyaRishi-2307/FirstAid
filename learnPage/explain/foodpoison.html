Food Poisoning

Prevent dehydration and help the body recover safely

Introduction

Food poisoning is caused by eating contaminated food or drinking contaminated water. Harmful bacteria such as Salmonella and Escherichia coli (E. coli), viruses, parasites, or toxins can make a person ill. Symptoms often appear within a few hours, although some infections may take several days to develop.

Most cases are mild and improve within a few days with rest and plenty of fluids. However, severe food poisoning can lead to dehydration and other serious complications, especially in young children, older adults, pregnant women, and people with weakened immune systems.

Common Causes

  • Undercooked meat and poultry.
  • Raw eggs and unpasteurized milk.
  • Contaminated seafood.
  • Improper food storage.
  • Poor kitchen hygiene.
  • Contaminated fruits and vegetables.
  • Dirty drinking water.
  • Cross-contamination during food preparation.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Feeling sick or nausea.
  • Vomiting, sometimes bloodstained.
  • Stomach cramps and abdominal pain.
  • Diarrhoea.
  • Headache.
  • Fever and chills.
  • Weakness and tiredness.
  • Loss of appetite.

Danger Signs

  • Blood in vomit or stool.
  • Severe dehydration.
  • Persistent vomiting.
  • High fever.
  • Confusion or fainting.
  • Severe abdominal pain.
  • Symptoms lasting more than three days.
Emergency: Seek urgent medical help if symptoms become severe or signs of dehydration develop.

Immediate First Aid Steps

  1. Help the person lie down and rest.
  2. Provide plenty of water to prevent dehydration.
  3. Encourage frequent small sips if vomiting occurs.
  4. Use oral rehydration solutions to replace lost salts and minerals.
  5. Avoid fatty, spicy, or heavy meals.
  6. Monitor their breathing and level of response.
  7. Encourage light foods such as toast, rice, bananas, or crackers once symptoms improve.
  8. Seek medical advice if symptoms worsen.

Food Poisoning First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Give plenty of fluids.
  • Encourage rest.
  • Use oral rehydration solutions.
  • Eat bland foods when able.
  • Wash hands thoroughly.
  • Seek medical care if symptoms worsen.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not force large amounts of food.
  • Do not drink alcohol.
  • Do not consume dairy products initially.
  • Do not ignore signs of dehydration.
  • Do not share towels or utensils.
  • Do not delay medical treatment if blood appears in vomit or stool.

Signs of Dehydration

  • Dry mouth and lips.
  • Extreme thirst.
  • Dark yellow urine.
  • Reduced urination.
  • Dizziness and weakness.
  • Sunken eyes.
  • Rapid heartbeat.
  • Confusion in severe cases.

Possible Complications

Severe food poisoning may cause dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, kidney failure, blood infections, or damage to the digestive system. Young children, elderly people, and individuals with weakened immune systems are at greater risk of complications.

Prompt treatment and adequate fluid replacement greatly reduce the risk of serious illness.

People Most at Risk

  • Infants and young children.
  • Pregnant women.
  • Older adults.
  • People with diabetes.
  • People with weakened immune systems.
  • Individuals receiving chemotherapy.
  • People with chronic illnesses.

Prevention and Safety Tips

🧼

Wash hands before cooking.

🍗

Cook meat thoroughly.

🥛

Use pasteurized products.

❄️

Refrigerate food properly.

💧

Drink clean water.

🥗

Wash fruits and vegetables.

When to Seek Medical Attention

Medical help should be sought immediately if the person has severe dehydration, blood in vomit or stool, persistent high fever, severe abdominal pain, confusion, or symptoms lasting more than three days.

Infants, elderly adults, pregnant women, and people with weakened immune systems should be evaluated early because they are more likely to develop complications.

Why Early Treatment Matters

Most cases of food poisoning improve with rest and fluid replacement. However, dehydration can develop quickly, particularly when vomiting and diarrhoea are severe.

Early treatment helps replace lost fluids and electrolytes, prevents complications, and speeds recovery. Severe cases require prompt medical attention to avoid life-threatening consequences.
