Hanging, Strangling & Throttling

Protect the airway and restore breathing immediately

Introduction

Hanging, strangling and throttling are medical emergencies that occur when pressure around the neck blocks the airway and prevents oxygen from reaching the lungs. Lack of oxygen can rapidly cause unconsciousness, brain damage and death if prompt action is not taken.

These injuries may happen accidentally, during assaults, suicide attempts, or when clothing becomes trapped in machinery. In severe cases, damage to the cervical spine (neck bones) may also occur, so the casualty should always be handled carefully.

Main Causes

  • Hanging: Suspension of the body by a rope, wire or noose around the neck.
  • Strangling: Compression of the neck by a cord, scarf, clothing or other object.
  • Throttling: Squeezing of the throat using hands or fingers.
  • Accidents involving machinery or moving equipment.
  • Violence and assault.
  • Suicide attempts.
  • Sports or work-related accidents.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Marks or bruises around the neck.
  • Difficulty breathing or noisy breathing.
  • Grey-blue skin (cyanosis).
  • Swelling around the neck.
  • Prominent veins on the face.
  • Tiny red spots in the eyes or face.
  • Impaired consciousness.
  • Dizziness or confusion.
  • Loss of consciousness.
  • Absence of breathing in severe cases.

Possible Complications

Lack of oxygen can cause permanent brain damage within a few minutes. Neck injuries may also damage the spinal cord and lead to paralysis or death.

Important: Always assume that a neck injury may be present and avoid unnecessary movement.

Immediate First Aid Measures

  1. Call emergency medical services immediately.
  2. Support the body and carefully remove any constricting material from around the neck.
  3. Do not destroy ropes, knots or clothing that may be required as evidence.
  4. Avoid moving the casualty unnecessarily because spinal injuries may be present.
  5. Lay the person gently on the floor.
  6. Open the airway carefully and check breathing.
  7. Loosen tight clothing around the neck and chest.
  8. Monitor pulse and breathing continuously.
  9. Keep the casualty warm and reassure them if conscious.

If the Casualty Is Not Breathing

  • Open the airway carefully.
  • Check for breathing and pulse.
  • Begin CPR immediately if necessary.
  • Provide rescue breaths if trained.
  • Continue CPR until help arrives.
  • Use an AED if available.
  • Monitor response continuously.

If the Casualty Is Breathing

  • Place the person in the recovery position.
  • Keep the airway open.
  • Watch for vomiting.
  • Observe breathing and pulse regularly.
  • Prevent unnecessary neck movement.
  • Cover with a blanket to maintain body temperature.
  • Stay with the casualty until medical help arrives.

First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Call emergency services immediately.
  • Support the body while removing pressure from the neck.
  • Handle the casualty carefully.
  • Keep the airway open.
  • Begin CPR if breathing stops.
  • Monitor pulse and consciousness.
  • Preserve evidence if the injury involves violence.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do not panic.
  • Do not move the neck unnecessarily.
  • Do not leave the casualty alone.
  • Do not delay calling emergency services.
  • Do not give food or drinks to an unconscious person.
  • Do not remove knots or cut evidence unnecessarily.
  • Do not stop CPR until help arrives or breathing returns.

Prevention and Safety Measures

Keep loose clothing away from machinery.

Follow workplace safety rules.

Keep cords and ropes away from children.

Prevent violence and unsafe situations.

Seek help for mental health crises.

Learn CPR and emergency first aid.

Why Immediate Action Matters

Brain cells begin to die after only a few minutes without oxygen. Prompt removal of pressure around the neck, maintaining an open airway, and starting CPR when necessary can save a life and reduce the risk of permanent brain injury.

Even when the casualty appears to recover, hidden injuries to the airway, blood vessels, brain or cervical spine may still exist. Therefore, every victim of hanging, strangling or throttling should receive urgent medical evaluation and monitoring.
