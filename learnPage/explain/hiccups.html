Hiccups Treatment

Quick home remedies to stop hiccups and knowing when to seek medical help

Introduction

Hiccups are involuntary contractions of the diaphragm—the muscle that separates your chest from your abdomen and plays an important role in breathing. Each contraction is followed by a sudden closure of your vocal cords, which produces the characteristic "hic" sound.

They are usually harmless and go away on their own after a few minutes. Common triggers include eating too quickly, drinking carbonated beverages, consuming too much alcohol, or experiencing sudden excitement or emotional stress. However, if they persist, there are several simple home remedies you can try to reset the diaphragm nerve.

1. Try Home Remedies

There are many physical maneuvers that can help stimulate the vagus or phrenic nerves, which are connected to the diaphragm. Try these common techniques:

Breathing & Posture

  • Hold your breath: Take a deep breath in and hold it for about 10 to 20 seconds, then breathe out slowly. Repeat if necessary.
  • Breathe into a paper bag: Slowly breathe in and out of a small paper bag. (Never use a plastic bag).
  • Knees to chest: Sit comfortably and pull your knees tightly to your chest for two minutes to compress the chest.

Eating & Drinking

  • Drink water: Drink a glass of cold water quickly.
  • Gargle: Gargle with ice water for a few seconds.
  • Sugar: Put a teaspoon of granulated sugar on your tongue and let it slowly dissolve. Note: For a young child, use corn syrup instead to prevent choking.
  • Bite a lemon: Bite into a lemon or taste a drop of vinegar to stimulate the nerves with a sour shock.
Other unusual but effective methods: You can gently pull on your tongue, gently massage the carotid artery in your neck, or use smelling salts to create a sudden stimulus that interrupts the hiccup reflex.

2. When to See a Health Care Provider

While most hiccups are a minor annoyance, intractable hiccups (lasting over 48 hours) can be a sign of an underlying medical condition, such as nerve damage, central nervous system disorders, or metabolic issues.

  • Duration & Disruption: See a health care provider if the hiccups last more than 3 hours and are severe enough to interfere with eating, breathing, or sleeping.
Seek a doctor quickly if hiccups are accompanied by any of these red flags:
  • Severe stomach pain
  • Fever
  • Shortness of breath or severe difficulty breathing
  • Vomiting
  • Throat tightness
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Coughing up blood

Hiccups DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Try holding your breath for 10-20 seconds.
  • Sip ice-cold water slowly.
  • Distract yourself by focusing on a complex task.
  • Sit quietly and focus on slow, controlled breathing.
  • Consult a doctor if hiccups persistently disrupt your sleep.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do NOT drink carbonated beverages while trying to stop hiccups.
  • Do NOT eat large, heavy meals quickly.
  • Do NOT give hard sugar cubes or dry spoons of sugar to babies or toddlers (choking hazard).
  • Do NOT panic; anxiety can cause hyperventilation and worsen hiccups.

Prevention Tips

🍽️

Eat slowly and chew your food thoroughly.

🥤

Avoid excessive carbonated drinks or sodas.

🍷

Limit alcohol consumption.

🌶️

Avoid overly spicy foods that irritate the esophagus.

🧘

Manage sudden emotional stress or excitement.

🌡️

Avoid sudden temperature changes in food or drinks.
