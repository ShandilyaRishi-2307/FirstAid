Epilepsy & Seizures

Know the essential steps to keep someone safe during and after a seizure

Introduction

Below is a basic guide about what to do in case of a seizure. Epilepsy is a neurological condition, and seizures can vary widely in how they present.

The most recognizable type is the convulsive seizure, where the body stiffens (known as the tonic phase) followed by general muscle jerking (the clonic phase). Knowing how to react calmly and safely is vital to protecting the person from injury.

Seizure First Aid DOs and DON'Ts

✔ DOs

  • Remain calm and stay with the person.
  • Time the seizure from start to finish.
  • Protect from injury, especially by placing something soft under their head.
  • Roll into recovery position after jerking stops OR immediately if there is food, fluid, or vomit in the mouth.
  • Maintain privacy and dignity by asking bystanders to step back.
  • Observe and monitor their breathing closely.
  • Gently reassure the person until they are fully recovered.

✖ DON'Ts

  • Do NOT put anything in their mouth. (They cannot swallow their tongue, and you could break their teeth or block their airway).
  • Do NOT restrain the person. Let the seizure run its course.
  • Do NOT move the person unless they are in immediate, life-threatening danger (e.g., in traffic or near a fire).
  • Do NOT apply CPR during the seizure.

Special Circumstances: Wheelchairs, Car Seats, or Strollers

If a seizure occurs while the person is seated in a wheelchair, car seat, or stroller, taking them out could cause injury. Instead, follow these steps:

  • Leave the person in the chair with their seatbelt/harness properly fastened.
  • Lean the person slightly to one side to aid the drainage of any fluid, food, or vomit in their mouth.
  • Support their head and protect their airway as required.
  • After the jerking completely stops, carefully remove them from the chair and place them in the recovery position on the floor if possible or required.

When to Call an Ambulance

While many seizures resolve on their own, emergency medical intervention is sometimes critical. Call for an ambulance immediately if:

  • You are in any doubt about their condition.
  • You arrive after the seizure has already started.
  • An injury has occurred during the seizure.
  • Food or water is in their mouth during the seizure.
  • The seizure has occurred in water (pool, bath, etc.).
  • This is not an exhaustive list, but it is a starting point to help you consider your emergency response.
  • The seizure lasts longer than normal for that person.
  • The seizure lasts longer than five minutes.
  • Another seizure follows quickly after the first one.
  • The person is non-responsive for more than 5 minutes after the seizure ends.
  • The person has breathing difficulties after the jerking stops.
  • It is the person’s first known seizure.
Emergency Reminder: Never hesitate to dial emergency services if a seizure happens in water, lasts beyond 5 minutes, or affects a person's ability to breathe post-seizure.

Living with Epilepsy: Safety Tips

🆔

Wear a medical ID bracelet.

💊

Take prescribed medication on time.

🛌

Get adequate, regular sleep.

🚿

Take showers instead of baths.

🚫

Avoid known seizure triggers.

👥

Educate friends and family on first aid.
